## 2026-06-19

One of the major factors across all the agencies is going to be private credit for the foreseeable future. Specifically, the role that banks play in lending to non-bank financial institutions (NBFI). That will certainly come up in each agency meeting.

For S&P specifically: "We think that the rapid growth in this type of lending, including to private credit players, can raise questions about the quality of underwriting, counterparty risk assessments, and the ability to manage what is an operationally intensive type of lending. Such lending requires strong surveillance, a thorough understanding of collateral, and safeguards to ensure that such collateral is not being double-counted, that is, pledged elsewhere.

That said, we don't see this as a material systemic risk that could cause major widespread problems for banks. However, NBFI lending will likely continue growing at a robust pace. Furthermore, for some banks, NBFI lending as a proportion of total lending has grown well into the low double digits and sometimes into the mid-teens or higher."

European banks have limited exposure to private credit although it's growing. Asia-Pacific banks do not have much exposure to private credit and so really not as big of an issue as in the US.

S&P will also look at exposure to AI stocks with the risk that those companies see higher valuations because of an AI bubble.

Some pitfalls for US Banks include: "Below-trend growth in the economy. Weak growth could threaten asset quality and weigh on business activity.
- Regulatory and supervisory easing. It is unclear how changes the Trump administration and regulators are pursuing will balance efficiency and effectiveness versus less stringent oversight.
- Exposures to nonbank financial institutions (NBFIs). While we don't expect these loans to cause major problems for banks, recent defaults could portend greater-than-expected losses.
- Asset price volatility. Elevated asset prices have boosted fees and trading. An unexpected correction could weigh on revenue and add to counterparty risk."

"Improvements in capital ratios, lower stress capital buffers, and declines in unrealized losses have created room for capital releases. However, the extent of capital reductions will only become clearer once regulators update capital requirements."

"Banks' continued focus on costs should limit growth and help offset expenses associated with investments in technology and some increased business activity."

### Moody's

Turning now to Moody's, they're much more concerned about capital rule uncertainty and lower capital requirements. If banks are able to lower their capital levels due to the reduced requirements under Basel 3 Finalization and less severe stress testing requirements for CCAR banks (banks with >100bn in assets), they view this as a credit negative. Expect more strict questions on keeping capital levels higher in the face of reduced regulatory burden.

As with all the agencies, CRE is no longer a significant credit concern. The concerns are now shifting to consumer loan performance as the economy cools and lending to NBFI due to private credit underwriting concerns.

### Sovereigns

For sovereigns, rating analysts oddly enough include a lot of weight towards public perception and headlines around the country when considering upgrades in addition to the quantitative factors.

### Fitch — Bank (online / fintech, ~$50bn assets, new ratings engagement)

Looking at Fitch from a recent new issuer meeting, some of the general questions they were asking. This is specifically for an around 50 billion in asset bank that is more of a fintech, online, high growth type bank. These are all from a new ratings engagement but can be applicable for ongoing rating meetings as applicable.

- Who do you consider your competitors?
- How do you calculate LTV?
- Understand you're taking share from big banks but what about other online platforms?
- How are you differentiated from those platforms?
- What is your capacity to handle a really high volume of transactions?
- Did appetites change during particular periods of market volatility?
- Do you anticipate appetites to change when rates come down or go back up?
- How do you manage risks of growing fast?
- What happens when growth slows and you start to reach for growth?
- The pendulum is swinging back to deregulation with a new administration, with a looser environment. How do you see this opportunity?
- Is this a long-term opportunity?
- What is the real advantage of blockchain for real-time payments?
- Are you handling crypto custody?
- What are your emerging risks, interests, and background?

Turning to underwriting approach and credit risk management:

- There's been a lot of stress from the enforcement of Federal Student Loan Payments. Has this affected you?
- Any movement in loan modifications recently or over the past few years?
- How do you think about high credit limits? Some larger banks have gotten in trouble with credit cards that have higher credit limits.
- How does data collection compare to large banks?
- Do your competitors who also collect data — are credit stats shown for all loans originated, or just the ones retained on book?
- What's your operating range for capital?
- What contingent liquidity access do you have?
- Are there any capital raises assumed?

### Moody's — Bank (fintech-y, with loan offtake)

Notes from Moody's. This bank has some loan offtake — originates loans and sells them to a third party or securitization. Some of these are fintech-specific; some are offtake-program-specific; some apply more broadly.

- What is going to drive hockey-stick growth?
- When will this happen?
- Will the number of products eventually start to decrease as you grow the business?
- Are there areas where you don't fully own the tech end-to-end?
- What would you buy to grow / improve the business?
- How do you think about recurring fee revenue across market cycles?
- How has the mix shifted over time?
- Are customers blind to who owns the loan?
- Is the ultimate purchaser of the loans' credit box different than your own?
- Can they shut the spigot off in short order?
- What are the covenants specifically to the offtake providers?
- Is credit a covenant in the loan platform agreements?
- To what extent are you looking to broaden to other banks?
- Any limits from regulators around how fast you can grow the bank?
- What is in the contribution margin?
- Is there a funds transfer pricing framework that allocates deposit costs to each of your segments?
- What is the personal loan NCO trend line versus general?
- Who are your competitors?
- On the broker's side, is the goal to provide own products or open platform?
- Who are your tech platform's main competitors?
- On enterprise risk management, how has the risk management infrastructure or headcount grown relative to overall growth?
- Are there any efficiencies from AI?

You are in a unique situation and are moving from fintech to a full-fledged bank. Where are you in that evolution? Are you adopting feedback from regulators, looking at peers, or bringing in talent? Is crypto an area that you're looking to get into? How are you planning to play in that space?

On underwriting approach and credit risk management:

- Are all originations direct, or would you consider buying or sourcing from brokers?
- Why don't you do auto?
- What is the desired market share for mortgages three years out?
- How does this tie back to targeted balance sheet size?
- What is the minimum amount of buffer maintained above well-capitalized?
- Does the bank have access to the discount window?
- What's the average account size for deposits?
- Are you liability sensitive?
- How do you hedge interest rate risk?
- What is the starting point for your view on credit spreads?

### General playbook — Negative outlook / review for downgrade / credit watch negative

As a general rule, when a company is on negative outlook or on review for downgrade, recommend:

- CFO-level engagement on a quarterly basis.
- CEO-level appearance at annual meetings and ahead of important announcements.
- Increased cadence of communication — key to achieving a stable outlook.
- Bi-weekly check-ins with the credit analyst.
- Provide additional comfort and attention, in addition to being front-footed on providing data and postings.
- You want the rating analysts to feel like they are insiders — that helps them get more comfortable around stabilizing the credit.
- Always be prepared with robust materials that address their most important concerns.
- Resolution windows: negative outlook needs to be resolved in 18–24 months; review for downgrade or credit watch negative needs to be resolved within 90 days.
